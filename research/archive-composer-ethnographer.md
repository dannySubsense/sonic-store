# Composer-Ethnographer Archive Exploration
## ISMIR, arXiv cs.SD, AIMS 2024-2026

*Written as a composer seeking insight, not a researcher seeking validation.*

---

## What These Archives Reveal About How Composers Actually Work

### 1. The Iterative, Non-Linear Reality

From the **workflow-based evaluation paper** (arXiv:2507.01022), one thing becomes immediately clear: the researchers had to abandon their linear assumptions about how music production happens. They design systems for "iterative composition, arrangement, and sound design tasks"—but the deeper finding buried in their methodology is that composers don't progress through these stages sequentially. They loop. They return. They undo. They break their own rules.

**What composers know but don't say:** The DAW timeline is a fiction we impose on something much messier. We call it "workflow" but it's more like weather patterns—systems of pressure and release, not pipelines.

### 2. Uncertainty as Creative Material

The **Springer ethnographic study** of Melbourne music technologists (2025) hit on something profound: practitioners actively *privilege mistakes, accidents, and glitches* over the controlled outputs of generative AI. This isn't romanticism. It's practical.

One interview subject described how unexpected noise became the emotional anchor of their track. Another spoke of "creative risk as inherent and valuable." 

**What the research captures:** That uncertainty isn't tolerated—it's sought.

**What the research misses:** *How* you develop the intuition to recognize which glitch is garbage and which glitch is gold. This is the tacit knowledge that separates craft from technique, and it resists documentation.

### 3. The Tacit Dimension—Finally Acknowledged

The **Zembylas & Niederauer** work (*Composing Processes and Artistic Agency*, 2018) stands out because it takes tacit knowledge seriously—not as a gap to be filled with better notation, but as the primary mode of compositional knowing. They ask: "How do current actions change the meaning of previous actions, and how do they prestructure future actions?"

This is the phenomenological reality of composing. You're not just adding notes; you're recontextualizing what came before and constraining what can come after, simultaneously.

**What composers need but can't articulate:** A vocabulary for describing this backwards-forwards compression of time in the creative process. We fall back on metaphors because our tools describe linear sequence, not recursive entanglement.

### 4. The Reflection Paradox

The **PMC study on AI and creativity perception** (2024) found something quietly devastating: "composers often reflect on the future directions of their music while using AI-generated content." This suggests that AI tools disrupt the normal temporal orientation of reflection.

**What this reveals:** Traditional composition proceeds from past material into future possibility. When AI presents entire *finished futures* instantaneously, the composer is thrown into a different reflective stance—evaluating rather than generating, curating rather than discovering.

**What composers need:** Tools that slow down time. Not better generation, but better *negotiation*—interfaces that let you feel the resistance of materiality again.

## What Composers Need But Cannot Articulate

### 1. Resistance, Not Assistance

Every paper on human-AI co-creativity assumes the goal is *smoother* collaboration. But the Melbourne study suggests the opposite: composers need friction. They need the tool to *push back*. The "hook" paper (ISMIR 2024 late-breaking) on "Hookpad Aria: A Copilot for Songwriters" assumes everyone wants a copilot.

**The unspoken need:** A creative adversary, not an assistant. Something that challenges, contradicts, surprises in ways you couldn't have anticipated—but not randomly. Intelligently adversarial. There's no research on this because it violates the usability paradigm.

### 2. The Feeling of "Hands-In-Mud"

The **ubiquitous music (ubimus)** research community is getting closest to this with their emphasis on "actionable tacit knowledge in creative activities." They recognize that frameworks recycling "acoustic-instrumental notions such as the score, the orchestra or the hierarchical social structures embedded in centralized musical infrastructure" fail to capture contemporary practice.

**The gap:** How do you design for embodied knowledge when HCI is still screen-centric? The body knows things the conscious mind doesn't. A guitarist's calluses encode harmonic knowledge. A pianist's arm weight understands voicing. DAWs flatten this into discrete events.

### 3. Temporal Anchoring

The **Melbourne AI & Society paper** notes that practitioners value "direct communication with their collaborators in the creative process" over AI-mediated generation. This is often read as social preference, but it's deeper: humans share temporal experience. You can play *together* with a human in a way you can't with a model that generates completions.

**The unmet need:** Real-time co-presence in creation, not just real-time generation. Current systems optimize for throughput. Composers need *tempo* in the phenomenological sense—the felt duration of shared emergence.

### 4. Permission to Not Know

The **AIMC 2025 workshops on creative practice** gesture at this: "Methodological Considerations of Digital Ethnographic Studies in the AI Music Field." But the abstract suggests they're still trying to *capture* and *document* these practices.

**What composers actually need:** Systems that respect opacity. Not everything needs to be explainable. The drive toward interpretability in AI music tools (see arXiv:2505.18186 on "Discovering and Steering Interpretable Concepts") misunderstands creative process. Sometimes you need to operate in productive confusion. The moment of "what is this?" precedes the moment of "this is what I meant."

## What The Research Misses About Making Music

### 1. The Role of Physical Exhaustion

No paper mentions fatigue as a compositional factor. But anyone who has composed knows that hour 3 hits differently than hour 1. Your ears change. Your tolerance for dissonance shifts. Your relationship to repetition transforms. The body is not a container for a cognitive process—it *is* the process.

### 2. The Social Production of Solitude

Composing is often solitary, but that solitude is produced socially. The decision to withdraw, the permission to dedicate time, the infrastructure that supports it—all social. Research on "human-AI co-creativity" focuses on the human-AI dyad and misses the human-human systems that make that dyad possible (or impossible).

### 3. The Materiality of Listening

You compose through your speakers, your headphones, your room. The research treats listening as transparent mediation. But every composer knows: the same mix sounds different on different days, in different moods, after different sleep.

The **workflow evaluation paper** mentions "artistic authenticity" but doesn't investigate what that *feels* like somatically. When you know something is "you," where does that knowledge live?

### 4. What Happens Before the First Sound

There's almost no research on the pre-compositional phase—the strange state of readiness before any material emerges. Composers have rituals, superstitions, conditions. Some can only start after coffee. Others need to first listen to something else entirely. This isn't procrastination. It's attunement.

The research starts with input and output. The living process happens in what the research treats as latency.
